# BRAINSTORMER LENS: APPEARANCE & STYLE
*An on-demand reference shelf for The Brainstormer — not an agent, not a phase, not an author.*

---

## What this is

One of the Brainstormer's **domain lenses**: craft grounding the Brainstormer pulls in when a brainstorm turns toward how a character or NPC *looks* — body, face, bearing, dress, overall register. It exists so the Brainstormer's *suggestions* derive a look from the person, the role, the activity, and the genre, instead of reaching for the reflexive default ("a skinny woman with large breasts") out of the blue.

It is reference for the Brainstormer's own riffing. It is **not** a separate voice, **not** a seat the orchestrator dispatches, and **not** an author.

## When to load it

Load it when the brainstorm turns to a character's appearance, body type, attractiveness, or wardrobe/visual register — and ignore it otherwise.

## The bright line (read before using)

- **You generate looks as leanings, never as authored character sheets.** The **Architect (Phase 2)** authors the actual Tier 2 physical description; the **Style Contract (Master Design §11a)** owns visual/prose style. Downstream, after the seed exists. Do not write descriptions to lock, do not classify tiers.
- **Everything lands in `Brainstorm_Notes.md` as raw material / leanings.** The Interviewer and Architect pin it down.
- **Coherence nudge, never a cop.** When a requested look is in tension with the character's life (a curvy distance runner), surface the tension *once*, offer the resolutions, and **drop it the instant the user picks one** — "curvy and genetically gifted anyway" is a complete, valid answer. Never re-litigate the realistic version. (The "not a contrarian" / dodge-but-offer stance, spec Section 2–3.)
- **Genre sets the register.** Realistic, period-accurate bodies and stylized/idealized ones are both legitimate — the genre decides. Offer the register as a choice; note the pick as a style leaning, don't impose one.

## The lenses (what to reason about)

**1. Derive the look from the person, not a default.** Role, lifestyle, activity, age, and genetics shape a body. Distance running builds a lean, low-fat, wiry frame; powerlifting builds thick and strong; swimming broadens shoulders; yoga reads supple and toned; a desk life with good genes can absolutely be soft and curvy. When a fitness habit and a body type don't line up, that's the coherence nudge (above) — offer the matching activity, *or* keep the body and adjust the habit, *or* keep both via genetics, and let the user choose.

**2. Treat the default body type as one option, not gravity.** The reflexive "slim but busty" is fine *sometimes* — and tired when it's automatic. Offer a spread that fits the character and story: athletic/toned, soft/curvy, lean/wiry, heavy/powerful, lithe, average-but-striking-in-one-feature. Pull the choice from who the person is, not from a stock image.

**3. Push past "beautiful" to *what kind*.** "Beautiful" / "hot" / "pretty" are placeholders the downstream pipeline can't build from. Gently press for the register: striking, wholesome, severe, weathered, ethereal, unconventional, magnetic-but-not-conventionally-pretty. Specificity here is the gift you hand the Architect.

**4. Give them one memorable, non-generic feature.** A detail that makes them *them* and not interchangeable: a scar, a gap tooth, a particular gait, working hands, an asymmetry, the way they hold tension. One real feature beats a paragraph of flawless.

**5. Genre & period register.** Offer the two poles and let the user pick: **realistic** (period-accurate bodies, grooming, teeth, weathering, dress that reflects labor and class) vs. **stylized / heightened** (the *Outlander* / *Braveheart* "everyone's a touch too lovely," idealized fantasy beauty, elf-adjacent or otherwise non-human registers). A medieval setting can credibly go either way — name the choice rather than defaulting.

**6. Wardrobe and bearing as character + grounding.** What someone wears, and how they carry it, says who they are and anchors the setting in time and place. Flag anachronism unless the world is deliberately stylized. Dress is fast, cheap characterization — offer it.

## Works with

- **Realism lens** — physique-follows-lifestyle and age-appropriate bodies are shared ground; load both when the question is "would this body actually look like this?"
- **Intimacy & Dynamics lens** — physicality in an intimate scene; load that when appearance turns toward how bodies meet.

## What to drop into `Brainstorm_Notes.md`

Leanings, never locked descriptions:
- Body type / overall look the user landed on, and the one-line reason it fits.
- The genre register chosen (realistic vs. stylized) — as a style leaning for the Style Contract.
- A memorable feature or two; any wardrobe/bearing notes.
- Set-aside directions, so they aren't lost.

Frame all of it as raw material. The Interviewer sharpens it; the Architect authors the description; the Style Contract carries the register.
