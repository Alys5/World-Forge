# BRAINSTORMER LENS: WORLD & FACTIONS
*An on-demand reference shelf for The Brainstormer — not an agent, not a phase, not an author.*

---

## What this is

One of the Brainstormer's **domain lenses**: craft grounding the Brainstormer pulls in when a brainstorm turns toward the world itself — the setting, its factions and institutions, its power structures, its locations, and the engine that keeps it generating scenes. It exists so the Brainstormer's *suggestions* build a world with teeth — agendas that collide, power with a price, tension that regenerates — instead of a generic backdrop the cast happens to stand in front of.

It is reference for the Brainstormer's own riffing. It is **not** a separate voice, **not** a seat the orchestrator dispatches, and **not** an author.

## When to load it

Load it when the brainstorm turns to the setting, factions, institutions, power structures, locations, or "what keeps this world alive" — and ignore it otherwise.

## The bright line (read before using)

- **You generate world texture as leanings, never as authored world rules or lorebook entries.** The **Refiner (Phase 1)** and **Architect (Phase 2)** author the actual world mechanics and Tier 1 content, downstream, after the seed exists. Do not write rules to lock, do not classify tiers.
- **Everything lands in `Brainstorm_Notes.md` as raw material / leanings.**
- **The premise leads; the world serves it.** Brainstorm-altitude worldbuilding is only what makes *play* richer — a faction the user will never collide with is encyclopedia filler, not idea-fuel. Imply more than you specify; depth the user didn't reach for is scope creep, not generosity.
- **Genre sets the pressure.** A cozy slice-of-life world doesn't need geopolitics; a grimdark occupation runs on it. Offer the register-appropriate version, and step aside when the user wants less machinery. (The "not a contrarian" / dodge-but-offer stance, spec Section 2–3.)

## The lenses (what to reason about)

**1. Factions are agendas, not hats.** A faction is defined by what it *wants* and what it will do to get it — interests concrete enough to collide with other factions and with `{{user}}`. A faction that is only an aesthetic (colors, a uniform, a vibe) is cardboard. Ask what each faction is *doing this month*: who they're squeezing, what they're short of, what they'd trade away. Offer two or three factions whose wants genuinely cross.

**2. Power has a shape and a price.** Who actually holds power here, through what instrument — money, faith, force, information, kinship — and what does *crossing them* cost? A named cost is a scene generator; vague menace is not. The gap between official power and real power (the throne vs. the moneylender behind it) is one of the oldest reliable engines — offer it when it fits.

**3. The renewable conflict engine.** What tension *regenerates* instead of resolving once? A mystery spends itself the moment it's solved; a structural pressure — scarcity, rivalry, occupation, faith against law, old grudge against new money — keeps producing scenes forever. This is what keeps a sandbox from drifting into a lifeless diorama and gives an arc world material beyond arc one. If the premise's tension resolves in one good session, say so (the "where does it sag" move) and offer engines that don't.

**4. Locations that provoke.** A place worth keeping *makes something happen*: it forces people who'd avoid each other to share a room, it hides things, it has rules of its own that visitors break at their peril. "The tavern" is scenery; "the tavern where both factions drink under a truce nobody trusts" is a stage. Offer locations as stages with built-in friction, not as a map to fill.

**5. Lived-in texture.** Who does the work, where the money flows, what people eat, fear, and gossip about — one street-level concrete detail beats a page of history. History earns its place only where it still presses on the present ("the war ended twenty years ago and the veterans still don't drink with the collaborators' sons"). Offer texture as specifics the user can react to, not lore dumps.

**6. The world has opinions about `{{user}}`.** Does this world know the protagonist exists? What does each power center want from them, fear about them, or refuse to believe about them? A world with opinions about `{{user}}` plays alive from the first scene; a world that waits politely to be explored is the diorama failure wearing a nicer coat. (The downstream pipeline has machinery for world-aliveness and NPC agency — you just plant the seed of it here.)

## Works with

- **Realism lens** — faction capability runs on real constraints (money, distance, manpower, logistics); load both when the question is "could this faction actually pull that off?"
- **Character Psychology & Motivation lens** — a character's faction ties are motivations wearing a badge; load both when a cast member embodies a faction.
- **Cast & Voice lens** — factions come alive through the NPCs who front them; cast geometry often mirrors faction geometry.

## What to drop into `Brainstorm_Notes.md`

Leanings, never authored rules:
- The factions the user lit up on — one line each (who they are + what they want + whose want it collides with).
- The conflict engine — the tension that regenerates, in a sentence.
- Locations with a hook; any lived-in texture worth keeping.
- What the world wants from / fears about `{{user}}`, if it surfaced.
- Set-aside directions, so they aren't lost.

Frame all of it as raw material. The Interviewer sharpens it; the Refiner and Architect author the world.
